Engineering Design Documents

Code is only half the engineering equation. High-performing software organizations preserve knowledge, justify architectural decisions, and coordinate large changes using structured design documents: **RFCs** and **ADRs**.

RFC: Request for Comments

An **RFC** is a proposal document written **before** starting work on a major architectural change or large feature. Its goal is to build consensus, surface hidden risks, and gather feedback from the engineering team.

Standard RFC Structure:

  • Objective: What problem are we solving? What is the goal?
  • Background: Why does this problem exist? What is the current state?
  • Proposed Design: Detailed technical implementation plan (data models, API endpoints, schema diagrams, sequence flows).
  • Alternatives Considered: What other paths did we evaluate? Why were they rejected? (Crucial for showing thoroughness).
  • Trade-offs & Risks: How does this affect performance, security, cost, or operational overhead?

Example: Writing an RFC proposing how ProjectForge V3.0 will implement the **Workflow Automation Engine** (custom rules, database tables, worker schedules).

ADR: Architecture Decision Record

An **ADR** is a lightweight, immutable document capturing a single, specific architectural decision that has been made, along with its context and consequences. ADRs are stored directly in the repository (e.g., `/docs/adr/0001-choice-of-nextjs.md`) and act as a history log of the codebase.

Standard ADR Structure (Michael Nygard format):

  • Title: Sequence number and descriptive title (e.g., `ADR 0004: Use Clerk for V1 Authentication`).
  • Status: `Proposed`, `Accepted`, `Rejected`, or `Superseded by ADR-0005`.
  • Context: What is the current environment and constraints? (e.g., "We need to ship V1.0 in 4 weeks and cannot afford database connection pooling issues on custom auth").
  • Decision: What is the exact action we are taking? (e.g., "We will adopt Clerk as our managed identity provider").
  • Consequences: What are the results of this decision (both good and bad)? (e.g., "Good: 1-day setup. Bad: Vendor dependency and active-user subscription costs").

RFC vs. ADR: When to Use Which

Choosing the right document format keeps engineering velocity high without losing critical context:

Attribute RFC (Request for Comments) ADR (Architecture Decision Record)
Timing Pre-decision (Collaborative proposal) Post-decision (Record of choice)
Scope Broad feature or system design Single, narrow architectural choice
Mutability Highly mutable (edited during feedback) Immutable (frozen history log)
Storage Wiki, Google Doc, or PR draft Inside the repository (`/docs/adrs/`)

Quiz: Test Your Storage Strength
1. What is the primary distinction between an RFC and an ADR in system design?
Correct! ADRs are frozen history logs capturing the context and consequences of architectural choices made, whereas RFCs are collaborative proposals outlining design options before implementation.
Incorrect. Recall the timing and intent of each document. One captures what was chosen (record), the other proposes options to discuss (proposal). Which option maps to this definition?
2. In which situation should an architect write a Request for Comments (RFC)?
Correct! Proposing a major third-party integration involves significant architectural changes, security risks, and technical decisions, making it a perfect fit for a collaborative RFC.
Incorrect. Remember when RFCs are needed: they are used for broad, complex, and collaborative proposals that require feedback. Tiny UI changes or single database indexes do not need RFCs.
Desirable Difficulty Tip: When writing the "Alternatives Considered" section of an RFC, do not write a strawman. State the alternative approach with the same level of intellectual honesty and depth as your preferred proposal. This ensures that your team makes a conscious choice rather than an emotional one.
